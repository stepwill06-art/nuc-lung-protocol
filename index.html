📋 Overview

Ventilation/Perfusion (V/Q) imaging is a nuclear medicine procedure used to evaluate lung ventilation and perfusion in patients with suspected pulmonary embolism (PE), chronic lung disease, and other respiratory conditions.

Clinical Significance

  • Assesses regional lung ventilation and blood perfusion
  • Gold standard for PE diagnosis in patients with normal chest X-ray
  • Evaluates chronic lung disease, asthma, and COPD
  • Uses low radiation dose with Tc-99m radiopharmaceuticals

Procedure Overview

1. Pre-Procedure

Patient assessment, chest X-ray, informed consent, IV line placement

2. Ventilation

Aerosol inhalation via nebulizer, posterior imaging

3. Perfusion

IV radiopharmaceutical injection, multi-angle imaging

🏥 Pre-Procedure Preparation

Patient Assessment

Risk Stratification:
  • Evaluate pre-test probability using clinical criteria (Wells Score, D-dimer)
  • Determine if low, intermediate, or high probability for PE
  • Review contraindications and allergies

Baseline Imaging

Chest Radiograph (within 1 hour):
  • Obtain PA and lateral chest X-ray
  • Essential for V/Q interpretation
  • Identifies cardiopulmonary abnormalities

Patient Preparation

  • Remove metallic objects and jewelry
  • Explain procedure and obtain informed consent
  • Establish IV access for radiopharmaceutical injection
  • Position patient supine or seated comfortably
  • Review breathing instructions
Assess for pregnancy or lactation. Consider alternative imaging if indicated.

Patient Instructions

Instruct patient: "You will breathe in a mist through a mouthpiece, then receive an IV injection. We will take pictures of your lungs from different angles. The procedure is painless and takes about 45 minutes."

💨 Ventilation Phase

Radiopharmaceutical

Tc-99m DTPA Aerosol

Activity: 25-40 mCi (0.925-1.48 GBq)
Route: Inhalation via nebulizer
Purpose: Assesses regional ventilation

Equipment Setup

Nebulizer Preparation:
  • Assemble disposable aerosol nebulizer per manufacturer instructions
  • Place Tc-99m DTPA into nebulizer reservoir
  • Connect to compressed air or oxygen source
  • Verify mouthpiece is clean and functioning

Imaging Technique

Positioning and Breath Hold:
  • Position patient: Upright in front of gamma camera
  • Anterior view preferred (posterior acceptable)
  • Ensure lungs are centered in camera field of view

Administration Procedure

  1. Instruct patient: "Take a deep breath and exhale normally"
  2. Introduce aerosol mist into breathing system
  3. Instruct patient: "Continue breathing the mist. Take a deep breath and hold it."
  4. Obtain anterior image during breath hold (10-20 seconds)
  5. Allow patient to breathe normally
  6. Acquire additional projections (posterior, obliques, if protocol requires)
Ensure adequate breath hold for sharp, diagnostic images. Coaching patient improves image quality.

Image Acquisition

View Duration Counts Notes
Anterior 10-20 sec 300K-500K counts Primary view
Posterior 10-20 sec 300K-500K counts May be omitted
Obliques Optional Same If protocol requires
Monitor patient for coughing or breathing difficulty. Allow rest if needed.

💉 Perfusion Phase

Radiopharmaceutical

Tc-99m MAA (Macroaggregated Albumin)

Activity: 5-10 mCi (0.185-0.37 GBq)
Route: Intravenous injection
Purpose: Assesses pulmonary perfusion
Mechanism: Microparticles lodge in pulmonary capillaries

Pre-Injection Preparation

Patient Positioning:
  • Position patient supine on imaging table
  • Ensure IV line is patent and functioning
  • Verify blood gas or pulse oximetry if indicated
Patient MUST be supine during MAA injection to ensure uniform distribution. Upright injection causes maldistribution to dependent lung bases.

Injection Technique

  1. Instruct patient: "You may feel slight warmth or mild chest tightness; this is normal and brief."
  2. Verify IV line placement in upper extremity
  3. Inject Tc-99m MAA slowly over 3-5 seconds while flushing
  4. Begin imaging immediately after injection
  5. Complete first image within 30 seconds of injection

Image Acquisition Protocol

Acquire eight static images at different angles around the thorax:

View Angle Duration Counts
Anterior 0° 15-30 sec 500K counts
LAO 45° 15-30 sec 500K counts
LL 90° 15-30 sec 500K counts
LPO 135° 15-30 sec 500K counts
Posterior 180° 15-30 sec 500K counts
RPO 225° 15-30 sec 500K counts
RL 270° 15-30 sec 500K counts
RAO 315° 15-30 sec 500K counts
Maintain patient immobility during imaging. Provide voice reassurance and alert button if anxiety develops.

Quality Assurance

  • Verify adequate radiopharmaceutical distribution
  • Check for unexpected uptake (liver, spleen, thyroid)
  • Assess image quality and count statistics
  • Confirm correct patient positioning for all projections

✓ Post-Procedure

Patient Care

  • Remove IV line if no additional imaging planned
  • Assess patient comfort and allow rest period
  • Provide post-procedure instructions
  • No restrictions on diet, activity, or driving
  • Routine hydration encouraged to promote renal clearance

Image Processing

Data Handling:
  • Transfer images to workstation
  • Apply routine attenuation and scatter corrections if available
  • Generate standardized multiplanar images
  • Archive images per HIPAA compliance

Interpretation Basics

Click to expand: Interpretation Guidelines

Reporting Categories (PIOPED II):

  • Normal/Near-Normal: No perfusion defects; PE unlikely
  • Low Probability: Ventilation/perfusion mismatch in <2 segments
  • Intermediate Probability: Moderate V/Q mismatch; clinical correlation needed
  • High Probability: One or more large segmental perfusion defects with normal ventilation

Key Interpretation Principles:

  • Mismatch Pattern: Perfusion defect with normal ventilation (suspicious for PE)
  • Match Pattern: Ventilation and perfusion defect together (suggests pneumonia, COPD)
  • Always compare with chest X-ray for clinical correlation

Reporting

  • Formal interpretation by qualified nuclear medicine physician
  • Report probability assessment for pulmonary embolism
  • Correlate with clinical presentation and chest radiograph
  • Recommend follow-up imaging if indicated (CT angiography)

Radiation Safety

Effective Dose: ~2-4 mSv (similar to 2-year background exposure)

  • No contact restrictions required
  • Inform patient regarding normal radiation clearance in 24-48 hours
  • Document in patient record

⚙️ Equipment & Materials

Required Equipment

Gamma Camera

Dual-head or multi-head system with low-energy, high-resolution collimator

Aerosol Nebulizer

Disposable system compatible with Tc-99m DTPA, with mouthpiece and filters

Imaging Workstation

Computer system for image reconstruction, processing, and archiving

IV Equipment

18-20 gauge IV catheter, normal saline flush, tourniquet, alcohol prep pads

Monitoring Equipment

Pulse oximeter, blood pressure cuff, oxygen if needed

Safety Equipment

Radiation detection meter, emergency supplies, lead shielding as needed

Radiopharmaceuticals

Agent Activity Route Purpose Half-Life
Tc-99m DTPA Aerosol 25-40 mCi Inhalation Ventilation imaging 6 hours
Tc-99m MAA 5-10 mCi IV injection Perfusion imaging 6 hours

Supplies Checklist

  • Patient gown and non-metallic items
  • IV tubing, syringes, needles
  • Sterile gauze and tape
  • Emergency medications (diphenhydramine, epinephrine)
  • Absorbent pads for table
  • Waste container for radioactive materials
