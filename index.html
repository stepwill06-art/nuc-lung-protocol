☢️ Nuclear Medicine Lung V/Q Protocol

Comprehensive Guide for Patients & Healthcare Providers

Welcome! 👋

You're scheduled for a lung imaging test (also called a V/Q scan or ventilation/perfusion scan). This guide will explain what to expect and help you prepare. Don't worry—this is a safe, painless test that takes about 45 minutes.

❓ What Is This Test?

This lung imaging test uses a special camera to take pictures of your lungs and see how well they're working. It has two parts:

Part 1: Breathing Test (5-10 minutes)

You'll breathe in a harmless mist through a mouthpiece. This mist helps the camera see how air moves into different parts of your lungs. You may feel a slight tickle in your throat—this is completely normal.

Part 2: Injection Test (30-40 minutes)

A small amount of radioactive dye will be injected into your arm through an IV. This dye flows through your blood to your lungs, and the special camera takes pictures showing how blood is circulating through them. You won't feel the dye—it's painless.

Total Time: About 45 minutes (varies by individual)

🏥 Why Do I Need This Test?

Your doctor ordered this test to check for:

  • Blood clots in the lungs (pulmonary embolism)
  • Breathing problems caused by lung disease
  • Areas of your lungs not getting enough blood flow
  • Other lung conditions that affect breathing and blood circulation

This test is particularly helpful because it's very accurate and uses less radiation than many other imaging tests.

This test is one of the safest ways to evaluate how your lungs are working.

✋ How to Prepare

Before Your Appointment

Important Checklist:
  • ✓ Tell your doctor if you're pregnant or think you might be
  • ✓ Tell your doctor if you're breastfeeding
  • ✓ List all medications you're taking
  • ✓ Tell us about any allergies
  • ✓ Bring your insurance card and ID
  • ✓ Arrive 15 minutes early to check in

What to Wear

Wear comfortable, loose-fitting clothes that are easy to remove (you may change into a hospital gown). Avoid clothes with metal buttons, zippers, or metal-lined pockets.

Food and Drink

You can eat and drink normally before your test. There's no need to fast or skip meals.

⏱️ What Happens During Your Test

Step-by-Step Timeline

1

Check-In & Paperwork (10 minutes)

You'll sign in at the front desk and complete a brief health questionnaire. Staff will confirm your information and answer any quick questions.

2

IV Line Placement (5 minutes)

A nurse will place a small IV (thin tube) in your arm. You may feel a brief pinch. This is how we'll give you the radioactive dye. A bandage will cover it.

3

Breathing Part of Test (5-10 minutes)

You'll be positioned in front of a special camera and asked to breathe in a mist through a mouthpiece. When you're told, you'll take a deep breath and hold it briefly while pictures are taken. This part is painless.

4

Rest & Injection (5 minutes)

You'll lie down on an imaging table. The technologist will slowly inject the radioactive dye through your IV. You won't feel anything unusual. Patients sometimes describe a very slight warm feeling—this is normal.

5

Imaging (20-30 minutes)

The camera will rotate around you taking pictures from different angles. You'll need to stay still and hold your breath occasionally. The technologist will give clear instructions. You can ask for breaks if needed.

6

Finish & Recovery (5 minutes)

The IV line will be removed. You can go about your day immediately. No special care needed.

🛡️ Safety & Radiation

Is This Test Safe?

Yes. This is a very safe test used worldwide for decades. Millions of patients have had this procedure without problems.

What About the Radioactive Dye?

The amount of radioactive material used is extremely small and leaves your body naturally through urine within 24 hours. It causes no harm.

Radiation Exposure

The radiation dose from this test is very low—similar to the natural radiation you'd receive in about 2 years from your environment.

After Your Test

  • You can go home right away—no restrictions
  • No special care needed
  • You can drive, work, and eat normally
  • Resume all normal activities
Drink plenty of water to help flush the dye from your body. It will leave naturally through urine in 24 hours.

❔ Frequently Asked Questions

Will the test hurt? ▼
No. The only thing you might feel is a brief pinch when the IV is placed in your arm. The breathing part and imaging are completely painless.
Can I eat or drink before the test? ▼
Yes. You can eat and drink normally. There's no fasting required for this test. Have a regular breakfast if your appointment is in the morning.
What if I'm claustrophobic? ▼
This test is very different from an MRI or CT scan. The camera rotates around you, but you're not enclosed or confined in any way. The room is open and comfortable. Tell staff if you have any anxiety—they can help.
Will I be radioactive after the test? ▼
No. You are not radioactive in any way. The tiny amount of dye used is quickly cleared from your body through normal urine. It's safe to be around family and friends immediately after the test.
How long will it take? ▼
Plan for about one hour total (45 minutes for the actual test plus 15 minutes for check-in). The actual imaging takes about 30-35 minutes.

📋 Clinical Overview

Ventilation/Perfusion (V/Q) imaging is a nuclear medicine procedure used to evaluate lung ventilation and perfusion in patients with suspected pulmonary embolism (PE), chronic lung disease, and other respiratory conditions.

Clinical Significance

  • Assesses regional lung ventilation and blood perfusion
  • Gold standard for PE diagnosis in patients with normal chest X-ray
  • Evaluates chronic lung disease, asthma, and COPD
  • Uses low radiation dose with Tc-99m radiopharmaceuticals

Procedure Overview

1. Pre-Procedure

Patient assessment, chest X-ray, informed consent, IV line placement

2. Ventilation

Aerosol inhalation via nebulizer, posterior imaging

3. Perfusion

IV radiopharmaceutical injection, multi-angle imaging

🏥 Pre-Procedure Preparation

Patient Assessment

Risk Stratification:
  • Evaluate pre-test probability using clinical criteria (Wells Score, D-dimer)
  • Determine if low, intermediate, or high probability for PE
  • Review contraindications and allergies

Baseline Imaging

Chest Radiograph (within 1 hour):
  • Obtain PA and lateral chest X-ray
  • Essential for V/Q interpretation
  • Identifies cardiopulmonary abnormalities

Patient Preparation

  • Remove metallic objects and jewelry
  • Explain procedure and obtain informed consent
  • Establish IV access for radiopharmaceutical injection
  • Position patient supine or seated comfortably
  • Review breathing instructions
Assess for pregnancy or lactation. Consider alternative imaging if indicated.

💨 Ventilation Phase

Radiopharmaceutical

Tc-99m DTPA Aerosol

Activity: 25-40 mCi (0.925-1.48 GBq)
Route: Inhalation via nebulizer
Purpose: Assesses regional ventilation

Equipment Setup

Nebulizer Preparation:
  • Assemble disposable aerosol nebulizer per manufacturer instructions
  • Place Tc-99m DTPA into nebulizer reservoir
  • Connect to compressed air or oxygen source
  • Verify mouthpiece is clean and functioning

Imaging Technique

Positioning and Breath Hold:
  • Position patient: Upright in front of gamma camera
  • Anterior view preferred (posterior acceptable)
  • Ensure lungs are centered in camera field of view

Administration Procedure

  1. Instruct patient: "Take a deep breath and exhale normally"
  2. Introduce aerosol mist into breathing system
  3. Instruct patient: "Continue breathing the mist. Take a deep breath and hold it."
  4. Obtain anterior image during breath hold (10-20 seconds)
  5. Allow patient to breathe normally
  6. Acquire additional projections (posterior, obliques, if protocol requires)
Ensure adequate breath hold for sharp, diagnostic images. Coaching patient improves image quality.

Image Acquisition

View Duration Counts Notes
Anterior 10-20 sec 300K-500K counts Primary view
Posterior 10-20 sec 300K-500K counts May be omitted
Monitor patient for coughing or breathing difficulty. Allow rest if needed.

💉 Perfusion Phase

Radiopharmaceutical

Tc-99m MAA (Macroaggregated Albumin)

Activity: 5-10 mCi (0.185-0.37 GBq)
Route: Intravenous injection
Purpose: Assesses pulmonary perfusion

Pre-Injection Preparation

Patient Positioning:
  • Position patient supine on imaging table
  • Ensure IV line is patent and functioning
Patient MUST be supine during MAA injection to ensure uniform distribution.

Injection Technique

  1. Inject Tc-99m MAA slowly over 3-5 seconds while flushing
  2. Begin imaging immediately after injection
  3. Complete first image within 30 seconds of injection

Image Acquisition Protocol

Acquire eight static images at different angles around the thorax:

View Angle Duration Counts
Anterior 0° 15-30 sec 500K counts
RAO/LAO 45°/315° 15-30 sec 500K counts
RL/LL 90°/270° 15-30 sec 500K counts
RPO/LPO 135°/225° 15-30 sec 500K counts
Posterior 180° 15-30 sec 500K counts
Maintain patient immobility during imaging. Provide voice reassurance and alert button if anxiety develops.

✓ Post-Procedure

Patient Care

  • Remove IV line if no additional imaging planned
  • Assess patient comfort and allow rest period
  • Provide post-procedure instructions
  • No restrictions on diet, activity, or driving
  • Routine hydration encouraged to promote renal clearance

Image Processing

Data Handling:
  • Transfer images to workstation
  • Apply routine attenuation and scatter corrections if available
  • Generate standardized multiplanar images
  • Archive images per HIPAA compliance

Interpretation Basics

Click to expand: Interpretation Guidelines

Reporting Categories (PIOPED II):

  • Normal/Near-Normal: No perfusion defects; PE unlikely
  • Low Probability: Ventilation/perfusion mismatch in <2 segments
  • Intermediate Probability: Moderate V/Q mismatch; clinical correlation needed
  • High Probability: One or more large segmental perfusion defects with normal ventilation

⚙️ Equipment & Materials

Required Equipment

Gamma Camera

Dual-head or multi-head system with low-energy, high-resolution collimator

Aerosol Nebulizer

Disposable system compatible with Tc-99m DTPA

IV Equipment

18-20 gauge IV catheter, normal saline flush, tourniquet

Radiopharmaceuticals

Agent Activity Route Purpose Half-Life
